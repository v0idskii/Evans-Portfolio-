06 Reflections
What I Learned
A situation that taught me an important lesson
Early in my placement, I was asked to help with a drywall repair and I cut a piece to the wrong measurement because I rushed through the measuring step. The piece didn't fit and had to be redone, which cost time on a tight schedule. My supervisor didn't yell he just showed me the right way, calmly. That moment taught me why tradespeople always say "measure twice, cut once." It sounds simple, but on a real job site, one mistake creates a chain reaction. I learned that slowing down and being precise is actually faster in the long run than rushing and having to redo work. Going forward, I double-check every measurement before I cut, and I've applied that same careful mindset to other tasks on site. This lesson will stay with me because it applies to so much more than drywall it's about taking your work seriously and respecting the time of the whole team.
Is construction suitable for you?
Yes, more than I expected. I genuinely enjoy physical, hands-on work and I like seeing something take shape over time. By the end of a shift, you can look at what you've done and feel a real sense of accomplishment. That feeling keeps me motivated.
What did you enjoy most?
I enjoyed the variety. No two days are exactly the same. I also liked being part of a crew — there's a real team dynamic on a construction site where everyone depends on each other to do their part.
What was surprising or difficult?
I didn't expect how much precision matters. I assumed construction was mostly just heavy lifting, but there's a lot of careful measuring, planning, and technique involved. The physical demands were also more intense than I anticipated at first, but I adapted quickly.
Was it what you expected?
Partly yes, partly no. I expected it to be demanding and physical, and it is. But I didn't expect to learn so many different skills so quickly, or to feel as respected by the crew as I did from early on.
How has this influenced your future plans?
This experience has confirmed that I want to pursue the skilled trades after high school. I'm now seriously looking into apprenticeship programs and trade school options for after graduation. Before co-op I wasn't sure; now I am.
Lasting memories
The memory I'll carry longest from this placement is when the crew took me out to eat on my birthday. I didn't expect it at all — these are busy, experienced tradespeople, and the fact that they took the time to do something like that meant a lot to me. It showed me that the people you work with can become more than just coworkers, and that a good workplace actually cares about the people in it. That moment made me feel genuinely valued, and it's something I won't forget.
07 Quote Response
In My Own Words
"A person who never made a mistake never tried anything new."
Albert Einstein, Scientist
Albert Einstein once said, "A person who never made a mistake never tried anything new," and my co-op experience at ICON Homes brought that idea to life in a very real way. When I first arrived on site, I was stepping into an environment completely outside my comfort zone — unfamiliar tools, experienced tradespeople all around me, and tasks I had never attempted before. It was almost guaranteed that I would make mistakes, and I did. Early on, I miscalculated a measurement that wasted material and slowed down part of our schedule. In the moment, I felt embarrassed, but my supervisor used it as a teaching opportunity rather than a reason to criticize me. That small failure became one of the most valuable lessons of my placement: slow down, be precise, and own your mistakes instead of hiding them. Einstein's quote resonates with me because construction is a field where trying new things is unavoidable — every site, every material, and every task has its own challenges. If I had been afraid to make mistakes, I never would have volunteered to try new tasks or pushed myself to improve. Over time, my errors became less frequent not because I stopped trying new things, but because I learned from each one. Going forward, whether in a trade apprenticeship, a future job, or any challenge in life, I plan to carry that mindset with me: mistakes are not failures — they are proof that I'm trying, growing, and not standing still.
